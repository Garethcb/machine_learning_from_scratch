Why Is It Naive?

Naive Bayes is naive because it assumes independence of input features. This means that the input features that go into our model are independent of one another (do not influence one another).

Example:

In spam filtering, the two messages below are treated the same due to the independence of each feature:

- "Dear Mike, you won".
- "Mike won, Dear you".

Understanding Conditional Probability

Consider a school with 110 students. These 110 students either prefer 'Soccer' or 'Golf' and are seen as either 'Females' or 'Males'.

Worked Example

$$\begin{array}{c|c} & \text{Female} & \text{Male} & \text{Total} \\ \hline \text{Soccer} & 31 & 41 & 72\\ \hline \text{Golf} & 9 & 29 & 38\\ \hline \text{Total} & 40 & 70 & 110 \end{array}$$

What is the conditional probability that a certain student likes ‘Golf’ given that he is a ‘Man’?

$$P(Golf|Male) = \frac{P(Golf \bigcap Male)}{P(Male)} = \frac{29}{70} = 0.41$$

Additionally, we can consider the conditional probability that a certain student is Male given that they like Golf.

Therefore, the Bayes Rule that we use for Naive Bayes, can be derived from these two notations:

$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \bigcap B)}{P(B)}$$$$P(B|A) = \frac{P(A \bigcap B)}{P(A)}$$

Bayes Theorom

$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A).P(A)}{P(B)}$$$$P(y|X) = \frac{P(X|y).P(y)}{P(X)}$$
- P(A|B) is the posterior probability
- P(B|A) is the likelihood of evidence: conditional porbability of X given Y is of a particular class
- P(A) is the prior
- P(B) is the Probability of evidence and it remains static for all classes
  1. The Bayes Rule allows us to use $P(X|Y)$ from our training dataset to find $P(Y|X)$.
- Replacing A and B with X (input features) and Y (target class)
  1. For our predictions based upon our testing set, our X is known while our target class is unknown. We can compute the probability of Y given X has already happened.

Worked Example

Golfing Dataset

$$\begin{array}{c|c} \text{Outlook} & \text{Windy} & \text{Played Golf} \\ \hline \text{Rainy} & False & N \\ \hline \text{Rainy} & False & Y \\ \hline \text{Sunny} & False & Y \\ \hline \text{Rainy} & True & Y \\ \hline \text{Overcast} & True & Y \\ \hline \text{Overcast} & False & Y \\ \hline \text{Sunny} & True & N \end{array}$$

Mathematical Walkthrough

$$P(y|X) = \frac{P(X|y).P(y)}{P(X)}$$$$X = (x_1,x_2,x_3,...,x_n)$$
  1. Variable y: is the class variable (played golf), which represents if it is suitable to play golf or not given the conditions.
  1. Variable X: represents our input features (Outlook and windy)
$$P(y|x_1,...,x_n) = \frac{P(x_1|y).P(x_2|y).P(x_3|y)...P(x_n|y)}{P(x_1).P(x_2).P(x_3)...P(x_n)}$$

We can now substitute the values from our training set into the equation. Notably, the denominator remains static. Therefore, the denominator can be removed and a proportionality can be introduced:

$$P(y|x_1,...,x_n) \propto P(y)\prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i|y)$$

For multiclass:

$$y = argmax_y P(y)\prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i|y)$$

Golfing Dataset

$$\begin{array}{c|c} \text{Outlook} & \text{Windy} & \text{Played Golf} \\ \hline \text{Rainy} & False & N \\ \hline \text{Rainy} & False & Y \\ \hline \text{Sunny} & False & Y \\ \hline \text{Rainy} & True & Y \\ \hline \text{Overcast} & True & Y \\ \hline \text{Overcast} & False & Y \\ \hline \text{Sunny} & True & N \end{array}$$

Played Golf

Prior:

P(A) = $\frac{Y}{Y+N}$ = $\frac{5}{7}$

Likelihood of evidence $P(y|x_1)$:

P(Played|Rainy) = $\frac{2}{5}$

P(Played|Sunny) = $\frac{1}{5}$

P(Played|Overcast) = $\frac{2}{5}$

Likelihood of evidence $P(y|x_2)$:

P(Played|Windy) = $\frac{2}{5}$

P(Played|Not_Windy) = $\frac{3}{5}$

Not Played Golf

Prior:

P(A) = $\frac{N}{Y+N}$ =$\frac{2}{7}$

Likelihood of evidence $P(y|x_1)$:

P(Not_Play|Rainy) = $\frac{2}{2}$

P(Not_Play|Sunny) = $\frac{1}{2}$

P(Not_Play|Overcast) = $\frac{0}{2}$

Likelihood of evidence $P(y|x_2)$:

P(Not_Play|Windy) = $\frac{1}{2}$

P(Not_Play|Not_Windy) = $\frac{1}{2}$

Will golf be played given that it is Sunny and Windy?

$$P(y|x_1,...,x_n) \propto P(y)\prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i|y)$$$$P(A).P(Played|Sunny).P(Played|NotWindy)$$$$y = \frac{5}{7}.\frac{1}{5}.\frac{3}{5} = 0.0857$$
$$P(y|x_1,...,x_n) \propto P(y)\prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i|y)$$$$P(A).P(\text{NotPlay|Sunny}).P(\text{Not Play|Not Windy})$$$$y = \frac{2}{7}.\frac{1}{2}.\frac{1}{1} = 0.0714$$

Since Play is greater than Not Play we can classify this observation as played.

Laplace Correction

$$P(A).P(NotPlay|Overcast).P(NotPlay|NotWindy)$$$$y = \frac{2}{7}.0.\frac{1}{1} = 0$$
  1. The value of P(Not_Play | Overcast) was zero in the above example.
  1. Therefore, when you have a model that includes this feature, the entire probability will become zero. To avoid this, we increase the count of the variable with zero to a small value.

Continuous Variables

Gaussian Naive Bayes

  • If we assume that the X follows a particular distribution, then you can plug in the probability density function of that distribution to compute the probability of likelihoods.
  • If you assume the X’s follow a Normal (aka Gaussian) Distribution, which is fairly common, we substitute the corresponding probability density of a Normal distribution and call it the Gaussian Naive Bayes. You need just the mean and variance of the X to compute this formula.
$$P(x_i|y) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma_y^2}} exp(-\frac{(x_i-y)^2}{2\sigma_y^2})$$

Likes Inception

Prior:

P(A) = $\frac{1}{2}$

Likelihood of evidence:

L(Popcorn = 9|Likes) = 0.021586

L(Candy = 63|Likes) = 4.802e-30

To avoid underflow (accuracy of caluclation with really small numbers) we log our likelihoods

$$Log(P(A).L(Popcorn = 9|Likes).L(Candy = 63|Likes))$$$$log(0.5)+log(0.021586)+log(4.802e-30) = -72$$

Disikes Inception

Prior:

P(A) = $\frac{1}{2}$

Likelihood of evidence:

L(Popcorn = 9|Dislikes) = 0.0519

L(Candy = 63|Dislikes) = 0.0119

To avoid underflow (accuracy of caluclation with really small numbers) we log our likelihoods

$$Log(P(A).L(Popcorn = 9|Dislikes).L(Candy = 63|Dislikes))$$$$log(0.5)+log(0.021586)+log(0.0119) = -5.78$$

Since the score for dislikes Inception is greater than Likes inception we will label this point as Dislikes Inception
